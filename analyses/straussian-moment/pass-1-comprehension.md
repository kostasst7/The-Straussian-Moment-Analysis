## Section 1: Introduction / The Question of Human Nature

### Backgrounder

#### The Enlightenment Project

The Enlightenment (roughly 18th century onward) was the broad intellectual movement that placed human reason, scientific inquiry, and individual liberty at the center of political and moral life. Its core assumptions about human nature include: (1) that people are fundamentally rational agents who respond to incentives; (2) that economic motivation is the primary driver of human behavior, making material prosperity the key to social stability; and (3) that history moves in a direction of progress, where better institutions, more commerce, and wider education will gradually pacify humanity. Under this framework, political philosophy could safely set aside questions about virtue, the soul, or the meaning of life and instead focus on institutional design, property rights, and the management of competing interests.

#### Athens vs. Jerusalem

Before the Enlightenment, the Western tradition drew on two deep and fundamentally incompatible sources:

- **Athens (classical philosophy):** Represented by thinkers like Plato, Aristotle, and later Machiavelli, this tradition emphasizes reason, civic virtue, and the life of the polis. The highest human achievement is the exercise of practical and theoretical wisdom. Political life demands courage, honor, and sometimes sacrifice; the good life is discovered through philosophy and enacted through citizenship.

- **Jerusalem (Christian theology):** Represented by the biblical tradition and thinkers like Augustine, this stream emphasizes faith, sin, grace, and salvation. The world is fallen; human beings are marked by original sin and cannot perfect themselves through reason alone. True fulfillment lies not in political achievement but in obedience to God; earthly kingdoms are, at best, tolerable arrangements in a vale of tears.

The tension is structural. The citizen of Athens asks: how do I live nobly and serve the city? The Christian of Jerusalem asks: how do I live faithfully and serve God? Each regards the other's sacrifices as vain. For the citizen, Christian mortification is a waste of energy that should go toward political and military excellence. For the Christian, the citizen's worldly ambitions are dust compared to eternal salvation.

#### Key Thinkers Referenced

- **Voltaire / Candide:** Voltaire (1694-1778) was a leading French Enlightenment writer and satirist. His novella *Candide* (1759) mocks philosophical optimism; the protagonist concludes that the wisest course is simply to "cultivate one's garden" -- a metaphor for modest, private contentment rather than grand ambitions.

- **Augustine (354-430):** The most influential theologian in Western Christianity. His *City of God* argues that earthly political orders are deeply flawed and that true peace belongs only to the heavenly city; human beings are fallen creatures prone to sin, and political life must reckon with this darkness.

- **Machiavelli (1469-1527):** Florentine political philosopher who broke with the classical-Christian synthesis by describing politics as it actually is rather than as it ought to be. He acknowledged human capacity for evil and argued that effective rulers must sometimes use force and deception -- a pagan realism about human danger.

- **Adam Smith (1723-1790):** Scottish philosopher and author of *The Wealth of Nations* (1776), the founding text of classical economics. He argued that self-interested economic activity, guided by markets, produces general prosperity -- placing economic rationality at the heart of political order.

- **Karl Marx (1818-1883):** German philosopher and author of *Das Kapital*. He agreed with Smith that economic forces drive history but argued that capitalism produces class conflict that will culminate in revolution. Despite their opposition, both Smith and Marx treat human beings as primarily economic actors.

- **Hegel (1770-1831):** German idealist philosopher who saw history as the progressive unfolding of reason and freedom, culminating in the rational state. He synthesized elements of both Athens and Jerusalem into a theory of historical progress.

- **Kant (1724-1804):** German philosopher who grounded morality in pure reason and envisioned perpetual peace through a federation of republican states. He represents the Enlightenment faith that rational principles can resolve political conflict without recourse to religious or traditional authority.

- **Hobbes (1588-1679):** English philosopher and author of *Leviathan*. Thiel identifies him as "the first truly modern philosopher" because he explicitly abandoned questions of virtue and the good life in favor of a single overriding concern: the avoidance of violent death. Hobbes argued that rational self-preservation, not honor or faith, should be the basis of political order.

- **Pierre Manent (b. 1949):** Contemporary French political philosopher. His book *The City of Man* traces how the mutual exhaustion of the classical and Christian traditions gave rise to the modern individual -- someone who defines himself by rejecting all forms of sacrifice.

#### The Early Modern Crisis

The tension between Athens and Jerusalem did not remain theoretical. In the 16th and 17th centuries, the Reformation shattered the institutional unity of Western Christianity. Martin Luther's challenge to the Catholic Church produced not peaceful reform but escalating violence, as Protestants and Catholics each tried to impose their vision of the true faith by force. The Counter-Reformation intensified the conflict. This escalation culminated in the **Thirty Years' War (1618-1648)**, fought primarily across the German lands, which by some estimates killed over half the population of Germany. It was one of the most devastating conflicts in European history.

The **Peace of Westphalia (1648)** ended the war by effectively agreeing to disagree: each sovereign would determine the religion of his own territory. This settlement is the founding moment of the modern state system. Its deeper consequence was that questions of virtue, the good life, and true religion were gradually pushed out of public life and into the private sphere. Polite, respectable people learned not to discuss them, because such discussions had led to catastrophic bloodshed. This is the political origin of the Enlightenment's "strategic retreat" from questions of human nature.

#### How This Sets Up the Essay

Thiel's argument is that September 11, 2001 shattered the Enlightenment framework by producing an adversary it could not explain. The 9/11 attackers were not the economically desperate masses that Marxist or liberal development theory predicted would turn to violence. They were affluent, educated, religiously motivated men who sought glory and martyrdom -- precisely the kind of human motivation that modern political thought had declared irrelevant or irrational. If economic incentives cannot contain violence, and if rational deterrence does not work against suicidal actors, then the Enlightenment's bargain -- safety in exchange for not asking dangerous questions about human nature -- has broken down. The rest of the essay asks what happens when those suppressed questions return, examining the problem through four thinkers: Locke, Schmitt, Strauss, and Girard.

### Summary

Thiel argues that the September 11 attacks exposed a fundamental failure in modern Western political thought. The Enlightenment had deliberately abandoned questions about human nature, virtue, and religion -- questions that once animated both classical philosophy and Christian theology -- because debating them had led to catastrophic violence (above all the Thirty Years' War). In their place, modernity substituted a framework built on economic rationality, material incentives, and institutional design. But the 9/11 attackers -- wealthy, educated, religiously motivated -- could not be explained or deterred by this framework. Their existence demonstrates that not all human beings are *homo economicus*, and that the political thought stretching from Smith through Marx to Kant and Hegel requires fundamental reexamination.

## Section 2: John Locke: The American Compromise

### Backgrounder

#### John Locke (1632-1704)

John Locke was an English philosopher and physician, widely regarded as the father of classical liberalism. Three of his works are central to this section:

- **Two Treatises of Government (1689):** Argues that legitimate government rests on the consent of the governed, that individuals possess natural rights to life, liberty, and property, and that political authority derives from a social contract. The Second Treatise develops his theory of property, arguing that labor is the source of value and that individuals create property by mixing their labor with the raw materials of nature.

- **An Essay Concerning Human Understanding (1689):** Argues that human knowledge is limited to what we can derive from sensory experience. We can know only the "secondary qualities" of things -- their observable effects and powers -- not their underlying substances or essences. In Thiel's reading, this epistemological claim is a deliberate demolition of the Aristotelian and Thomistic tradition that grounded political and moral life in claims about human nature.

- **The Reasonableness of Christianity (1695):** Ostensibly defends Christianity against atheism, but Thiel reads it as an exercise in esoteric writing: Locke redefines Christian teachings so thoroughly -- emphasizing self-preservation over love of God, loosening the obligations of marriage and filial piety, legitimating the accumulation of wealth -- that the Christianity that emerges is virtually indistinguishable from secular liberalism.

#### The American Founding and Locke

Thiel calls Locke the "definitive founder" of the United States -- not merely an influence on the founders but the architect of the conceptual framework within which America was conceived. The Declaration of Independence echoes Lockean language directly ("life, liberty, and the pursuit of happiness"), and the Constitution's framework of limited government and individual rights reflects Lockean principles. Thiel's provocative claim is that America is a Lockean experiment, and that the success of this experiment caused Americans to forget how radical the founding premises were.

#### Hobbes vs. Locke

Both Hobbes and Locke reject the older Aristotelian-Thomistic tradition and begin from a hypothetical "state of nature," arguing that rational self-interest drives individuals to form political societies. They differ on severity: Hobbes requires an absolute sovereign; Locke preserves individual rights. But in this section, Thiel treats them as part of the same project. Hobbes reduces all human motivation to the desire for power; Locke elaborates this reduction by stripping human attributes of anything specifically human -- will, liberty, and understanding become mere "powers," and the underlying substance that possesses them becomes unknowable.

#### Key Concepts

- **State of nature:** Locke's hypothetical pre-political condition, described as an "ill condition" of neediness and wretchedness. If the state of nature is nearly worthless, then almost all value comes from human labor, which justifies strong property rights and makes questions about original appropriation largely irrelevant.

- **Natural rights:** Rights that individuals possess prior to government. In Locke's framework: life, liberty, and property. Thiel argues these derive not from a positive account of human nature but from the *impossibility* of knowing human nature.

- **The unknowable "X":** Thiel's term for Locke's radical agnosticism about human essence. Because we cannot know what human beings truly are, we have no basis for telling them what to want or how to live -- and from this agnosticism, liberal rights paradoxically follow.

#### How This Section Connects to Section 1

Section 1 established the problem: the Enlightenment abandoned questions about human nature after the religious wars, substituting economic rationality. September 11 exposed this substitution's limits. Section 2 is the first of four thinker-specific examinations. Locke represents the Enlightenment's positive answer to the crisis described in Section 1 -- how the West actually built its post-religious-war order. Thiel treats Locke's achievement as genuinely impressive but exposes its internal limitations: the "boundary cases" (unjust property origins, religious actors, the non-Western world) that the Lockean framework defers or dismisses, and that 9/11 forced back into view.

### Summary

Thiel argues that John Locke constructed the intellectual framework for American liberal democracy by pursuing a deliberate rhetorical strategy: rather than openly attacking Christianity or the older philosophical tradition, Locke quietly redefined their core concepts until they became compatible with secular capitalism. At the epistemological level, Locke replaced the Aristotelian claim that we can know human nature with the claim that human nature is unknowable (the "X"), and from this agnosticism he paradoxically derived the strong liberal rights to religion, speech, and property. This framework proved enormously successful, but it depended on deferring certain "boundary cases" -- the justice of original property acquisition, people who reject economic rationality, the non-Western world. September 11 forced these deferred questions back into view, as bin Laden's religiously motivated violence, funded by oil wealth that refutes Locke's labor theory of value, demonstrated that the Lockean compromise cannot account for a world where not everyone has accepted its terms.

## Section 4: Leo Strauss: Proceed with Caution

### Backgrounder

#### Leo Strauss (1899-1973)

Leo Strauss was a German-Jewish political philosopher who fled Nazi Germany and settled at the University of Chicago, where he taught from 1949 to 1968. His intellectual project was a recovery of classical political philosophy against the crisis of modernity -- the collapse of the West's confidence in reason, natural right, and the possibility of genuine philosophical knowledge. Strauss argued that modern political philosophy, from Machiavelli through Hobbes, Locke, and Nietzsche, had progressively lowered the horizon of human aspiration, culminating in relativism and nihilism. His students have been controversially associated with American neoconservatism.

#### Esoteric vs. Exoteric Writing

Strauss's core hermeneutical method. He argued that from antiquity through the early modern period, serious philosophers wrote on two levels: an **exoteric** surface conforming to prevailing orthodoxies (for the public), and an **esoteric** level "between the lines" conveying heterodox views visible only to "trustworthy and intelligent readers." This was a rational response to the threat of persecution: Socrates was executed, and every subsequent philosopher had reason to remember.

#### Key Works Referenced

- **Persecution and the Art of Writing (1952):** Strauss's methodological work laying out the theory of esoteric writing. Thiel draws on its thought experiment of the historian in a totalitarian country and its famous line about the advantages of esoteric communication.

- **Natural Right and History (1953):** Strauss's most systematic work, attacking modern historicism and value relativism while arguing for the recovery of classical natural right. It directly opposes the Lockean "unknowable X."

#### The Katechon

A theological concept from 2 Thessalonians 2:6-7 -- the mysterious force "that restraineth" the coming of the Antichrist. In medieval political theology, often identified with the Roman Empire or the Church. Schmitt adopted it as a political category: the katechon is whatever order prevents the final dissolution of meaningful political life. Thiel applies it to Strauss's project, which aims to fortify the West by recovering knowledge of the "permanent problems" -- but which paradoxically may hasten the dissolution it seeks to prevent.

#### Founding Violence: Romulus/Remus, Cain/Abel

Both Roman legend and Genesis locate fratricide at the origin of civilization: Romulus kills Remus to found Rome; Cain kills Abel and builds the first city. Strauss draws attention to this convergence as evidence that civilizational origins are inseparable from primal violence -- a truth Locke's account of peaceful property acquisition deliberately obscures.

#### How This Connects

Sections 1-3 established a dilemma with no exit: Locke is too naive, Schmitt too violent. Strauss attempts a middle path -- recovering hard truths about human nature while avoiding Schmitt's openly violent solutions, using esoteric discretion (the "veil" over "sad exigencies"). But Thiel ultimately judges even Strauss's path as self-undermining.

### Summary

Thiel presents Leo Strauss as attempting the central synthesis of the essay: a path between Lockean naivete (which suppresses dangerous truths about human nature) and Schmittian violence (which would destroy the liberal order in the act of defending it). Strauss's key insight is that the great philosophers wrote esoterically -- encoding dangerous but genuine truths "between the lines" for careful readers only -- because certain truths about founding violence, the nature of the city, and the limits of justice in wartime are too destabilizing for open discourse. Against Locke's "unknowable X," Strauss maintains that a permanent truth about human nature exists and can be known, but only through painstaking study, never through systematic summary. However, Thiel argues that the Straussian project is self-undermining: the very act of recovering awareness of the "permanent problems" makes the unreflective defense of liberal democracy impossible. The section concludes by gesturing toward intelligence services and secret action as the practical domain where Straussian insights might be applied, but leaves unresolved whether such a path can sustain the liberal order or will instead accelerate its unraveling.
